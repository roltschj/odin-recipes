Spaghetti & Beans

OBLIGATORY CONTENT PRECEDING USEFUL INFORMATION

Ahhh... the joy of spaghetti & beans. The mere mention of the words elicits a multitude of reactions among the listener. Shock, awe, disgust, confusion. Occasionally, delight. It's become something of a meme among my inner circle. But I haven't been eating it for 15 years because it's funny. I eat it because it is simple, cheap, quick, nutritious, and delicious.

The year was 2008. I was living in my first apartment with two friends, suddenly paying for everything rather than nothing. In search of a staple meal that satisfied the five qualifications previously mentioned, I dreamed this up. Whole grains paired with legumes form a complete protein. Complex carbohydrates keep you going. Leafy green vegetables are always a winner. Add a sauce of some kind and for less than two bucks and twenty minutes, you're good for half a day. Additionally, it's a modular meal. Change it up to avoid boredom.

INGREDIENTS

  • 1 serving whole grain spaghetti
    • About the diameter of a quarter, when bunched in a circle. Go with your gut.
    • I use thin spaghetti or cappellini - it cooks quickly!
  • 1/2 can Black beans, rinsed
    • Alternatively, chickpeas, pinto, kidney... any kind of bean, really. It's up to you.
  • 4-8 oz. spinach, washed
    • Seriously, such a quantity will cook down to the size of a single leaf. Go wild.
    • Alternatively, collard greens, turnip greens, mustard greens... the possibilities are endless.
  • Sauce (see A Note On Sauces)

THE METHOD

  1. Bring a salty pot of water to a boil. While you're doing that, heat up a pan. Add a little bit of oil to both.
    • I use canola oil for its neutral flavor, but here, too, you can use anything you like. This is a judgment-free zone.
  2. Add spaghetti and beans to the boiling water. While it cooks, Saute the greens.
  3. While the grains, beans & greens are cooking, determine and actualize your sauce situation.
  4. When the spaghetti is cooked, drain it and return it to the pot.
  5. Mix in greens and sauce, and enjoy!

A NOTE ON SAUCES

Here is where the dish's flavor is determined. Cooked spaghetti and beans, in and of themselves, have a very neutral flavor. I will now discuss two options, but the subjective nature of taste, paired with the aforementioned modularity of this dish, necessitates and enables experimentation with various accoutrements to find your preferred flavor profile. If you're in a hurry, though, go with Option 1.

Option 1

Store-bought pasta sauce. Season to taste.

Option 2

  • 1/2 cup tahini
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1/3 cup nutritional yeast

Since tahini is quite thick, it is best to first mix it in with the pasta just after draining, as the high temperature will loosen it up a bit. So too will the soy sauce, but be careful about adding too much- an overabundance of sodium is never a good time.
